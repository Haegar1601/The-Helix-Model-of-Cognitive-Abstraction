## Overview

This repository contains a whitepaper that argues for a simple but consequential shift in how advanced AI systems should be understood and evaluated.

Most current benchmark paradigms measure isolated model performance under cold-start conditions. This whitepaper argues that such evaluations systematically miss a class of cognitive phenomena that only emerge through sustained interaction, accumulated context, and recursive human-AI calibration.

The central proposal is the **Helix Model of Cognitive Abstraction**, together with the **Helix Abstraction Level Scale (HALS)**: a framework for analyzing how abstraction depth can increase over time in extended human-AI dialogue.

The current manuscript line, v4.3, further clarifies the distinction between organically grown dialogue and statically imported context, and strengthens the paper's treatment of in-context calibration, persistent memory, and verification structure.

## Core Claims

- Benchmark results on isolated tasks are not sufficient to capture the highest abstraction behavior of frontier models.
- Some relevant cognitive performance is process-dependent rather than state-dependent.
- Under sustained interaction, the most meaningful unit of analysis may no longer be the human or the AI alone, but the coupled human-AI system.
- Persistent memory and real-time data access are not peripheral features; they materially affect what kind of cognition becomes possible.
- Deficit-based evaluation can fail both in AI benchmarking and in the assessment of neurodivergent cognition when the evaluator measures below the system's actual mode of strength.

## Why It May Matter

If the strongest forms of abstraction in advanced AI systems only become visible under sustained human-AI interaction, then some widely used evaluation assumptions may be structurally incomplete.

That matters for:

- frontier model evaluation
- memory architecture design
- human-AI product design
- AI safety and capability interpretation
- neurodiversity and cognitive assessment
